Water Cycle

Overview

The water cycle, also known as the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth.

Process

The water cycle consists of several main processes:

  1. Evaporation: The sun's heat causes water from oceans, lakes, and rivers to evaporate into the atmosphere in the form of water vapor.
  2. Condensation: As water vapor rises, it cools and condenses into tiny droplets, forming clouds.
  3. Precipitation: When the water droplets in clouds become heavy enough, they fall to the ground as precipitation, including rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  4. Runoff: Precipitation that falls on land can either soak into the ground (infiltration) or flow over the surface, eventually making its way back to rivers, lakes, and oceans.
  5. Transpiration: Plants absorb water from the soil through their roots and release it into the atmosphere through small openings in their leaves called stomata.

Importance

The water cycle is essential for maintaining life on Earth. It helps regulate the Earth's temperature, distributes freshwater resources, and sustains ecosystems.
